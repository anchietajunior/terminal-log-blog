Before LCD, before LED, there was phosphor. Monochrome CRT monitors were the first window into the digital world — and their amber glow remains one of the most iconic images in computing.

## The three colors of phosphor

Monochrome monitors came in three main variations, each defined by the type of phosphor used in the cathode ray tube:

| Type | Color | Typical use |
|------|-------|-------------|
| P1 | Green | General-purpose terminals |
| P3 | Amber/Orange | Word processing |
| P4 | White | Graphics applications |

**Amber phosphor (P3)** was considered the most comfortable for long work sessions. The warm color reduced eye strain, and many operators preferred these monitors for 8-hour shifts or longer.

## The IBM 5151

Released in 1981 alongside the original IBM PC, the 5151 was a green monochrome monitor that defined an era. With a resolution of 720x350 pixels in text mode, it displayed 80 columns by 25 rows — the standard we still use today in many terminals.

## The legacy

When we open a terminal today — whether it's iTerm, Windows Terminal, or any emulator — we're using a direct descendant of those machines. The metaphor of a blinking cursor on a dark background is one of the most enduring interfaces in the history of technology.

There's something almost meditative about this interface. No notifications, no banners, no distractions. Just you, the cursor, and the possibility of creating anything.
